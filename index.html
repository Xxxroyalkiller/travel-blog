Overview

A Nation Unlike
Any Other

South Korea sits in East Asia, sharing the Korean Peninsula with North Korea to its north and bordered by the Yellow Sea to the west and the Sea of Japan to the east. It is home to over 51 million people, with roughly half living in the greater Seoul metropolitan area — one of the largest urban zones on Earth.

What makes South Korea extraordinary is the scale of its transformation. Within a single generation, the country rose from one of the world's poorest nations to a global powerhouse of technology, entertainment, and design. Today it's home to Samsung, Hyundai, BTS, and Parasite — globally recognized names spanning hardware, music, and film.

Yet beneath this modern surface lies an ancient, deeply preserved culture. Confucian traditions, Buddhist temples, and centuries-old architecture are not relegated to museums — they are woven into daily Korean life. That tension between old and new is exactly what makes it so compelling to experience as a traveller.

Cuisine

Eat Your Way Through Korea

Korean food is bold, communal, and deeply satisfying. It is built around sharing — a table full of banchan (small side dishes) alongside a central dish is the Korean way. Here are the six essential dishes to seek out:

Kimchi
김치

Fermented vegetables — most often napa cabbage — seasoned with chili paste, garlic, and ginger. Served as a side dish at virtually every meal. A UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage since 2013.

Korean BBQ
한국식 바베큐

Grilled meats — bulgogi (beef), samgyeopsal (pork belly), and galbi (short ribs) — cooked right at your table over charcoal or gas. One of the most interactive and social dining experiences in the world.

Bibimbap
비빔밥

A vibrant mixed rice bowl topped with seasoned vegetables, a fried egg, gochujang (chili paste), and often beef or tofu. Mix everything together before eating — the name literally means "mixed rice."

Tteokbokki
떡볶이

Chewy cylindrical rice cakes simmered in a sweet and spicy gochujang sauce. Korea's most beloved street food — found at every pojangmacha (street stall) and night market across the country.

Fried Chicken
치킨

Double-fried for maximum crunch, then glazed with sauces ranging from sweet soy garlic to fiery yangnyeom. Often paired with draft beer in a combination so iconic, Koreans call it chimaek.

Japchae
잡채

Stir-fried glass noodles made from sweet potato starch, tossed with a colourful mix of vegetables, mushrooms, and beef in a sesame-soy sauce. A staple of Korean celebrations and festive occasions.

Currency & Money

South Korean Won (₩)

South Korea's currency is the Korean Won (KRW). The numbers look large but are easy to work with once you get used to them. Cards are accepted nearly everywhere in cities, but carry cash for street stalls and traditional markets.

💡 Money Tips for Korea:

Get a T-Money card (tap-to-pay transit card) on arrival — it works on subways, buses, and even some convenience stores. Myeongdong street stalls and Namdaemun Market are cash-preferred. Most convenience stores (7-Eleven, CU, GS25) have ATMs that accept international cards.

Must-See Places

8 Places You
Cannot Miss

01

Gyeongbokgung Palace

Seoul's grand royal palace, founded in 1395 under the Joseon Dynasty. The guard-changing ceremony (hourly, 10am–3pm) is a living piece of history. Rent a hanbok nearby to enter free.

Seoul
02

N Seoul Tower

Perched atop Namsan Mountain at 479m above sea level, this iconic tower offers 360° panoramic views of the entire Seoul skyline. The cable car ride up is an experience in itself.

Seoul
03

Bukchon Hanok Village

A beautifully preserved hilltop neighbourhood of 600-year-old traditional Korean homes (hanok). Wander the narrow stone-paved alleys for a window into pre-modern Seoul.

Seoul
04

Myeongdong Shopping Street

Seoul's most vibrant commercial district — K-beauty brands, street food stalls, fashion boutiques, and neon lights in every direction. Buzzing at any hour, day or night.

Seoul
05

Lotte World Adventure

One of the world's largest indoor theme parks, combining thrilling rides with a Magic Island outdoor section beside Seokchon Lake. A full day of entertainment for all ages.

Seoul
06

Gamcheon Culture Village

Busan's "Santorini of Korea" — a hillside maze of colourful, artist-decorated homes and murals. Once a refugee settlement, now a thriving outdoor art museum and community space.

Busan
07

Haedong Yonggungsa Temple

One of Korea's most unusual temples — built directly on coastal cliffs above the East Sea. The sound of waves crashing on the rocks while the morning light hits the golden pagodas is unforgettable.

Busan
08

Jagalchi Fish Market

Korea's largest seafood market, stretching along Busan's harbour. Buy fresh catches directly from vendors and have them prepared on the spot. An essential, immersive local experience.

Why South Korea?

More Than a Destination —
A Chance to Grow

South Korea checks every box for the kind of traveller I am. As a niche tourist, I'm not here to follow a pre-packaged tour or tick off the same Instagram spots as everyone else. I want something real: authentic local interactions, experiential learning, and the chance to understand a culture through living inside it, even briefly.

The contrast that Seoul represents draws me in deeply. You can stand in a centuries-old palace courtyard in the morning, watching the Joseon-era guard-changing ceremony, and be lost in a futuristic underground shopping mall by afternoon. That tension between old and new isn't just interesting to observe — it forces you to think about what culture actually means, and how identity persists through change.

The food alone would justify the trip. Korean cuisine is communal by design — it's impossible to eat Korean food and not connect with the people around you. That embodies my entire philosophy of travel: use food, use language, use experience to close the distance between yourself and the world.

On a deeper level, Korea's entertainment and youth culture — K-pop, K-dramas, the entire Hallyu wave — has already shaped how a generation of young people around the world see art and identity. To visit the source of that influence and understand it in its own context is something genuinely meaningful.

"South Korea isn't just a destination. It's a place where ancient wisdom and electric modernity coexist — and I want to stand in the middle of that."

As my code of ethics demands: travel with purpose, respect the culture, leave every place better than you found it. South Korea demands nothing less, and gives so much in return.
